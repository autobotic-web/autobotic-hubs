1 Segments, digits & I2C

Each digit is built from seven LED bars labelled a–g. Light the right ones and they form a number — a "7" is just bars a, b and c.

Four digits would mean wiring 28 LEDs — way too many pins. So a driver chip, the HT16K33, handles them all, and the Pi just sends data over I2C:

🔌 I2C uses only two signal wires: SDA (the data line) and SCL (the clock that keeps both sides in step). It's the same "save your pins" idea as the button matrix.

🧰 Vocabulary 7-segment display — a digit made of seven LED bars (a–g) you switch on/off. HT16K33 — the driver chip that controls all the LEDs for you. I2C — a two-wire way for chips to talk: SDA (data) + SCL (clock). Library — ready-made code you import; here, Adafruit's Seg7x4. zfill(4) — pads a number with leading zeros so 42 becomes "0042".

🐛 Bug Hunt — this countdown shows "42" then "41" squished to the left instead of "0042". Click the broken line:

for num in range(9999, -1, -1):
    display.print(num)
    time.sleep(1)
Why that line? display.print(num) sends the raw number, so short numbers don't fill all four digits (and print wants a string). Use display.print(str(num).zfill(4)) so 42 shows as 0042. Formatting the value before showing it is the key habit.
