1 What's a vibration motor?

A vibration motor is a tiny motor with an off-balance weight on its shaft. When it spins, the wobble shakes the whole device — that's the buzz you feel.

Two things to know:

⚡ The Pi's GPIO pin can't supply enough current to spin a motor directly (that's fine for an LED, not a motor). The OiviO board gives it the power; your code just sends the on/off signal.
🎚️ It's an output (an actuator): code commands it, it acts — unlike the LDR and PIR sensors, which read the world.

🧰 Vocabulary Vibration motor — a motor with an off-balance weight that shakes instead of spinning a wheel. Actuator / output — a part that acts on the world (motor, LED, buzzer), the opposite of a sensor. Current — the "flow" of electricity; motors need more of it than a GPIO pin can safely give. Haptic — feedback you feel (a buzz or tap), not see or hear. Duration — how long something lasts; here, how long the motor stays on (set by sleep()).

2 Patterns of buzzes

The real skill is controlling how long the motor buzzes. A short buzz and a long buzz are all you need to send a message — that's exactly how Morse code works: · dot (short) and — dash (long).

In code, a dot is on() → sleep(0.1) → off(), and a dash is the same with a longer sleep, like sleep(0.4). Change the duration, change the meaning.

🐛 Bug Hunt — this should buzz for 2 seconds, but the motor barely twitches. Click the broken line:

motor.on()
motor.off()
sleep(2)
Why that line? motor.off() runs the instant after on(), so the buzz is over before you feel it — and the sleep(2) just waits with the motor already off. The fix: put the sleep(2) between on() and off(), so it holds the buzz. Order matters!
