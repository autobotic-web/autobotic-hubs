1 Hue, Saturation, Value

HSV splits a colour into three: Hue (which colour), Saturation (how vivid), Value (how bright). The big win: when light changes, mostly the Value shifts — the Hue stays put. So you can lock onto a colour even as lighting changes.

To find a colour you: convert BGR→HSV, then cv2.inRange(hsv, lower, upper) makes a mask — white where pixels fall in your range, black elsewhere. Tune the range with trackbars until only your object is white.

🧰 Vocabulary HSV — Hue (colour), Saturation (vividness), Value (brightness). Hue — the colour itself; OpenCV uses 0–179 (red≈0, green≈60, blue≈120). Mask — a black/white image: white = matched, black = not. inRange — builds the mask from a lower & upper HSV range. Trackbar — an OpenCV slider you drag to tune values live.

🐛 Bug Hunt — the mask is all black no matter what. Click the broken line:

lower = np.array([40, 50, 50])
upper = np.array([20, 255, 255])  # Hue max below min!
mask = cv2.inRange(hsv, lower, upper)
Why that line? The upper Hue (20) is below the lower (40), so the range is empty and no pixel can match — the mask is all black. Make the upper Hue ≥ the lower (e.g. lower 20, upper 40). inRange needs lower ≤ upper.
