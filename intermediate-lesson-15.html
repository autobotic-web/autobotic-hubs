1 How it works: ping & echo

The sensor shouts a sound you can't hear, then times the echo bouncing back. Press the button and watch one "ping" go out and return:

📡🧱
Press the button to send one ping.
The sound goes to the wall and back — that's a round trip. So we cut the time in half (÷ 2) to get the one-way distance. Sending takes the same path as returning, like a tennis ball hitting a wall and rolling back to you.

📖 New words, in plain English

  • Ultrasonic — sound that's too high-pitched for humans to hear (like a dog whistle). The sensor uses it so the room stays silent to us.
  • Pulse — one short burst of that sound, like a single hand-clap.
  • Echo — the pulse bouncing back off an object, the same way your shout bounces off a far wall.
  • TRIG (trigger) — the pin that tells the sensor "shout now". It's the mouth.
  • ECHO — the pin that listens for the bounce-back. It's the ear.

🐛 Bug Hunt: this alarm lights the LED even when nothing is near it. Read it like a detective and click the line with the bug:

distance_cm = sensor.distance * 100
print(f"Distance: {distance_cm:.1f} cm")
if sensor.distance < 20:
    led.on()
Why that line? sensor.distance is in metres, so sensor.distance < 20 means "closer than 20 metres" — almost always true, so the LED never switches off. It should use the cm value: if distance_cm < 20:. Same idea as setting a "warn me within 20 km" alarm for a parking spot — it beeps the whole journey.
